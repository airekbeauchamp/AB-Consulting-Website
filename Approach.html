The Scholar-Practitioner Difference

Most communication consultants come from one of two backgrounds: academic theory with no real-world application, or business experience with no intellectual depth. I bring both.

My PhD in writing studies and sound studies isn't just credentials on a wall. It's hands-on research into how marginalized communities use voice and sound to navigate systems, claim authority, and resist being ignored. This isn't abstract theory—it's directly applicable intelligence about how authentic communication cuts through noise and commands attention.

Seven years building and scaling communication programs taught me that systems beat scattered tactics every time. I secured $365,668 in competitive funding not by writing impressive proposals, but by understanding what funders actually want to see versus what applicants think they want to hear.

Four Principles That Drive Everything

1. Real Expertise Over Generic Polish

The market is oversaturated with polished content that could be selling anything. What's rare—and what people pay premium prices for—is genuine expertise delivered with authentic voice.

Instead of asking: "How do we make this sound more professional?" I ask: "What do you actually know that others don't, and how do we showcase that knowledge consistently?"

2. Systems That Scale Without Losing Soul

One-off tactics don't build businesses. Neither do rigid systems that strip away everything interesting about how you actually think and work.

I create frameworks that grow with you while preserving what makes your expertise valuable. Whether you're writing to one person or one thousand, the voice remains authentically yours.

3. Platform Integration, Not Platform Optimization

Most consultants optimize for individual platforms: "Here's your LinkedIn strategy. Here's your email strategy." But your audience doesn't live in silos, and neither should your communication.

I help you build ecosystems where every piece reinforces your expertise. Your newsletter supports your speaking topics, which reinforce your client work, which feeds back into your thought leadership.

4. Demonstration Over Performance

There's a crucial difference between communication that performs expertise and communication that demonstrates it. Performance is about appearing knowledgeable. Demonstration is about actually being useful.

One creates the impression of expertise. The other provides evidence of it. Guess which one builds lasting business relationships?

The Methodology: How We Actually Work

Phase 1

Expertise Archaeology

Before writing a single word or choosing a single platform, we dig deep into what you actually know. This isn't a questionnaire or brand audit template. It's strategic analysis:

  • Knowledge mapping: What do you understand that others miss?
  • Experience inventory: Which problems have you solved?
  • Credibility audit: What evidence do you have of real expertise?
  • Market positioning: Where does your knowledge create the most value?
Phase 2

Systems Architecture

Based on your actual expertise and business goals, we design communication systems that work together:

  • Message hierarchy: Core themes consistent across contexts
  • Platform strategy: Where your knowledge creates most impact
  • Content frameworks: Systems that demonstrate expertise while saving time
  • Integration planning: How each piece reinforces the others
Phase 3

Implementation and Optimization

Strategy without execution is expensive advice. But execution without strategy is just busy work:

  • Voice documentation: Guidelines that maintain authenticity
  • Workflow creation: Systems that produce quality efficiently
  • Training and support: Team development for consistent execution
  • Performance measurement: Metrics that track business impact
Phase 4

Scaling and Evolution

Good communication systems evolve with your expertise and business goals:

  • Regular refinement: Quarterly reviews based on results
  • Expansion planning: How to grow reach without diluting message
  • Team development: Building capacity while maintaining quality
  • Market adaptation: Adjusting to changes while preserving core positioning
